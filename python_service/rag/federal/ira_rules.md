# IRA (Individual Retirement Account) Rules - 2025

## Overview
An IRA is a personal retirement account you open yourself (not through employer). There are two main types: Traditional IRA and Roth IRA.

## 2025 Contribution Limits

| Age | Limit |
|-----|-------|
| Under 50 | $7,000 |
| 50 or older | $8,000 (includes $1,000 catch-up) |

**Note:** This is the COMBINED limit for Traditional + Roth IRA. You can split between them, but total cannot exceed $7,000 (or $8,000 if 50+).

## Traditional IRA

### Tax Treatment
- **Contributions**: May be tax-deductible (depends on income & 401k status)
- **Growth**: Tax-deferred
- **Withdrawals**: Taxed as ordinary income

## Comparison: Traditional vs Roth IRA

| Feature | Traditional IRA | Roth IRA |
|---------|-----------------|----------|
| Tax deduction now? | Maybe (income limits) | No |
| Tax on growth? | Deferred | FREE |
| Tax on withdrawal? | Yes (ordinary income) | FREE |
| Income limit to contribute? | No | Yes ($165k single) |
| Income limit to deduct? | Yes ($89k single w/401k) | N/A |
| Required minimum distributions? | Yes, at age 73 | No |
| Best for | Lower income now, higher later | Higher income now |

## Common Questions

### Q: Can I have both 401(k) AND IRA?
**A: YES!** They are completely separate accounts with separate limits.
- 401(k): $23,500 limit
- IRA: $7,000 limit
- Total possible: $30,500/year in tax-advantaged retirement savings!

### Q: I have 401(k) and make $109,000. Can I deduct IRA?
**A: NO.** Your income ($109k) exceeds the $89k limit for single filers with 401(k). 
**HOWEVER**, you CAN contribute to Roth IRA (no deduction, but tax-free growth)!

### Q: What's better - more 401(k) or add IRA?
**A:** First, get full employer 401(k) match (free money!). Then:
- If income < $89k: Traditional IRA (deductible)
- If income $89k-$165k: Roth IRA (tax-free growth)
- If income > $165k: Max 401(k), then Backdoor Roth

### Q: Can I contribute to both Traditional AND Roth IRA?
**A: YES**, but the $7,000 limit is COMBINED. Example:
- $4,000 Traditional + $3,000 Roth = $7,000 total ‚úÖ
- $5,000 Traditional + $5,000 Roth = $10,000 ‚ùå (over limit!)

### Q: What if I contribute to Traditional IRA but can't deduct it?
**A:** This is called "non-deductible Traditional IRA" - generally NOT recommended because:
- Pay tax now (no deduction)
- Pay tax later (on growth)
- Roth IRA is better: Pay tax now, NO tax later!
