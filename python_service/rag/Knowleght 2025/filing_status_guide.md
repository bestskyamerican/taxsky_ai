# Filing Status Guide - 2025

## The 5 Filing Statuses

| Status | Code | Who Qualifies |
|--------|------|---------------|
| Single | S | Unmarried, no dependents |
| Married Filing Jointly | MFJ | Married, filing together |
| Married Filing Separately | MFS | Married, filing apart |
| Head of Household | HOH | Unmarried with dependent |
| Qualifying Surviving Spouse | QSS | Widow(er) with dependent child |

---

## 1. Single

### Requirements
- Unmarried on December 31
- OR legally separated/divorced
- OR spouse died before Jan 1 of tax year
- Do NOT qualify for HOH or QSS

### 2025 Tax Benefits
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Standard Deduction | $15,000 |
| 10% bracket up to | $11,925 |
| 12% bracket up to | $48,475 |

---

## 2. Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)

### Requirements
- Legally married on December 31
- Both spouses agree to file jointly
- Both report ALL income

### 2025 Tax Benefits
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Standard Deduction | $30,000 |
| 10% bracket up to | $23,850 |
| 12% bracket up to | $96,950 |

### Advantages of MFJ
✅ Lowest tax rates (usually)
✅ Higher standard deduction
✅ Higher income limits for credits
✅ Can claim all credits (EIC, education, etc.)
✅ Only one return to file

### Disadvantages
❌ Both liable for all tax, interest, penalties
❌ Can't separate income if one spouse owes IRS

---

## 3. Married Filing Separately (MFS)

### Requirements
- Legally married on December 31
- Choose to file separate return

### 2025 Tax Benefits
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Standard Deduction | $15,000 |
| 10% bracket up to | $11,925 |
| 12% bracket up to | $48,475 |

### When MFS Makes Sense
✅ Protect yourself from spouse's tax problems
✅ Spouse owes back taxes (IRS won't take your refund)
✅ Keeping finances separate (divorce pending)
✅ One spouse has high medical expenses (7.5% AGI threshold lower)
✅ Income-based student loan repayment

### Disadvantages of MFS
❌ CANNOT claim EIC (Earned Income Credit)
❌ CANNOT claim education credits (usually)
❌ CANNOT claim adoption credit
❌ Child tax credit reduced
❌ SALT deduction capped at $5,000 (not $10,000)
❌ Lower IRA deduction limits
❌ 85% of Social Security may be taxable
❌ If spouse itemizes, YOU must itemize

### Special Rule
If you live APART from spouse for last 6 months of year:
- May be able to file as HOH
- Can claim EIC

---

## 4. Head of Household (HOH)

### Requirements (ALL must be met)
1. **Unmarried** on December 31 (or "considered unmarried")
2. **Paid more than half** the cost of keeping up a home
3. **Qualifying person** lived with you more than half the year

### Qualifying Persons for HOH
| Person | Live with You? | Must Be Dependent? |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| Child (son, daughter, step, foster) | Yes, 6+ months | Usually yes |
| Parent | NO (can live elsewhere) | Yes |
| Other relative | Yes, 6+ months | Yes |

### "Considered Unmarried" Rule
You can file HOH if married but:
- Lived apart from spouse for last 6 months of year
- Paid more than half of home costs
- Home was main home for your child for 6+ months
- You claim child as dependent (or could, if not for custody rules)

### 2025 Tax Benefits
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Standard Deduction | $22,500 |
| 10% bracket up to | $17,000 |
| 12% bracket up to | $64,850 |

### HOH is Better Than Single!
```
Example: $60,000 taxable income

Single tax:      $8,817
HOH tax:         $7,442
Savings:         $1,375!
```

---

## 5. Qualifying Surviving Spouse (QSS)

### Requirements (ALL must be met)
1. Spouse died in 2023 or 2024 (for 2025 return)
2. You did NOT remarry before end of 2025
3. You have a dependent child
4. Child lived with you all year
5. You paid more than half of home costs
6. You could have filed MFJ in year spouse died

### How Long Can You Use QSS?
- Year spouse died: File MFJ (or MFS)
- Year 1 after death: QSS
- Year 2 after death: QSS
- Year 3+: Single or HOH (if qualify)

### 2025 Tax Benefits
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Standard Deduction | $30,000 |
| Tax brackets | Same as MFJ |

## Decision Tree: Which Status?

```
START: Are you married on Dec 31?
│
├─ YES → Do you want to file together?
│        │
│        ├─ YES → Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)
│        │
│        └─ NO → Did you live apart last 6 months?
│                 │
│                 ├─ YES, with child → Head of Household (HOH)
│                 │
│                 └─ NO → Married Filing Separately (MFS)
│
└─ NO → Did your spouse die in last 2 years?
         │
         ├─ YES → Do you have dependent child?
         │        │
         │        ├─ YES → Qualifying Surviving Spouse (QSS)
         │        │
         │        └─ NO → Single or HOH
         │
         └─ NO → Do you have a dependent?
                  │
                  ├─ YES → Did you pay 50%+ of home costs?
                  │        │
                  │        ├─ YES → Head of Household (HOH)
                  │        │
                  │        └─ NO → Single
                  │
                  └─ NO → Single
```

## Common Mistakes

### ❌ HOH Errors
- Filing HOH when married (must be "considered unmarried")
- Child didn't live with you 6+ months
- Didn't pay 50%+ of home costs
- Claiming non-qualifying person

### ❌ MFJ Errors
- Filing jointly when divorced (check Dec 31 status)
- Not reporting spouse's income
- Not both signing return

### ❌ MFS Errors
- Not itemizing when spouse itemizes
- Claiming credits you're not allowed (EIC, etc.)
- Forgetting spouse's SSN on return

---

## Special Situations

### Same-Sex Marriage
- Recognized for federal tax purposes
- File as MFJ or MFS if legally married

### Common-Law Marriage
- Recognized in some states
- If legally married under state law, file as married

### Divorce Pending
- Status on Dec 31 determines filing status
- If not finalized, you're still married

### Spouse Died During Year
- Can file MFJ for that year
- Then QSS for 2 years (if child dependent)

### Nonresident Alien Spouse
- Cannot file MFJ (usually)
- Unless elect to treat as resident for tax purposes

---

## Changing Filing Status

### Changing FROM Joint to Separate
- Can amend within 3 years
- File Form 1040-X

### Changing FROM Separate to Joint
- Can only change before due date (including extensions)
- After due date: CANNOT change to joint

### Changing TO HOH
- Can amend if you discover you qualify
- Common when realizing "considered unmarried" rule
